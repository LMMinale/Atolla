The Science
What is a mesophotic reef?+
A mesophotic reef — or mesophotic coral ecosystem (MCE) — is the deep, light-limited zone of a coral reef found roughly between 30 and 181 metres. It sits below the depth most divers and most reef science reach, yet still receives enough light to support corals, gorgonians, sponges and fish. Mesophotic reefs are among the least-explored ecosystems on Earth, which is exactly why the Atolla Research Initiative focuses there.
How deep does the team dive, and why does depth matter?+
The team works to a maximum depth of 181 metres. Depth matters because most coral reef research stops at around 30 metres. The mesophotic zone below that is where many unique, undescribed species live — and where signals of reef change can appear that aren't yet visible in the shallows. Reaching it reliably requires specialist closed-circuit rebreather diving.
What are gorgonian octocorals?+
Gorgonians are a group of soft corals — also called sea fans and sea whips — in the subclass Octocorallia. They are a dominant and diverse feature of mesophotic reefs and sit at the centre of the initiative's taxonomy, phylogenomics and new-species discovery work, with more than 8,000 gorgonian specimens curated to date.
What is CCR (closed-circuit rebreather) diving?+
A closed-circuit rebreather recycles a diver's exhaled gas — removing carbon dioxide and adding oxygen — which allows far longer and deeper dives than conventional scuba. CCR diving is what makes sustained scientific work at mesophotic depths of 50 to 181 metres possible.
Why do mesophotic reefs matter for conservation?+
Mesophotic reefs hold significant biodiversity, may act as refuges for shallow-reef species under stress, and remain largely undocumented. Understanding them is essential to protecting reef ecosystems as a whole — and the species and signals found there can inform climate, conservation and ocean-policy decisions.
The Initiative & the People
What is the Atolla Research Initiative?+
The Atolla Research Initiative is an independent marine science programme that documents and protects mesophotic reef ecosystems between 30 and 181 metres across the Indo-Pacific. It combines closed-circuit rebreather diving with cutting-edge phylogenomics to discover new species and understand deep reefs before they are lost.
Who is Dr Sonia J Rowley?+
Dr Sonia J Rowley is an evolutionary marine biologist and closed-circuit rebreather diving pioneer with over 40 years of diving experience. She specialises in gorgonian octocoral taxonomy and mesophotic reef ecology, and is affiliated with the Scripps Institution of Oceanography, the Natural History Museum London, and the Smithsonian Institution.
Where does the initiative work?+
Field sites span the Indo-Pacific, including Pohnpei and surrounding atolls in Micronesia, Lombok and Wakatobi in Indonesia, and Timor-Leste — across more than twelve active mesophotic research sites, several with a decade-long time series.
The Data
What data does the initiative collect?+
The dataset documents mesophotic reef ecosystems from 30 to 181 metres and includes voucher gorgonian specimens, environmental DNA (eDNA) water samples, high-definition underwater video and imagery, standardised dive transects, and oceanographic data such as temperature, salinity and dissolved oxygen. See The Data for the full breakdown.
Is the dataset open access? What licence applies?+
Yes. The field dataset is released under a Creative Commons CC BY 4.0 licence — free for academic use with attribution. Commercial use is available by arrangement. A DOI is issued on release so the dataset can be cited directly.
How can researchers access or cite the data?+
On release, the dataset is available through open repositories with a citable DOI, and eDNA records are submitted to public biodiversity databases. For collaboration, co-authorship, or early access ahead of release, contact the team.
Support & Get Involved
How can I support or fund the research?+
The initiative is independently funded, so contributions directly extend the number of survey days completed each season. You can fund a dive day, become a research partner, or support the open-data programme — supporters receive field dispatches and early access to findings. Start on the Get Involved page.
Can I join an expedition or volunteer?+
Yes. There are ways to support expeditions and to take part in the citizen-science programme, which helps classify species and footage from dives. Get in touch and we'll find the right fit.
How can institutions or companies partner?+
Institutions and companies working in marine science, climate research, or ocean technology are invited to join as formal research partners. Partnerships can include co-authorship and access to the full expedition dataset. Reach out through the Contact page.
How can my organisation access or work with this ocean data?+
Organisations working in conservation, climate reporting, ocean policy, or operations can access first-hand sub-surface research data through the initiative. If that describes you, the short confidential survey below helps shape how this data is made available — or contact the team directly to discuss partnership.
